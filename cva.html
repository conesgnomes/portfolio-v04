Our client, a biotechnology company, was coming to market with a revolutionary new cancer treatment. They tasked us with creating an iPad app that could be used by company reps when speaking with doctors to educate them about how the process works, the benefits of it, as well as display its safety and outcome data in an easy to digest way.

All content has been blinded to maintain client confidentiality.

Problem

Our client wanted a digital tool that would allow their reps to quickly and easily walk through all aspects of their new product with doctors. Reps have no more than 30 minutes to spend with doctors, so the tool needed to present complex data and information while being simple to navigate so they can customize their presentation to the individual doctor they are speaking with.

The content and visual look and feel of this app would to serve as the base for all of the other digital pieces that our client would be releasing alongside it for their product launch. This included separate patient- and doctor-facing websites, email campaigns, and digital banner ads.

Solution

Our app allows for reps to quickly jump to specific content sections so they can tailor their conversations to be uniquely relevant to each doctor they speak with since, for example, one doctor may be more concerned with safety while another is focused more on success rates.

We built a design system that could be used across our client's entire digital ecosystem. This included charts, graphs, tables, and infographics alongside the expected UI components.

Scope and constraints

This project needed to be designed and developed in just under 8 months to ensure it went live alongside the launch of the cancer treatment. Built into this timing was four rounds of three-week long legal reviews to ensure all claims, data, charts, and graphs passed the FDA’s stringent requirements for medical accuracy.

Design

The design process began with outlining the app's requirements as supplied by our client and their legal team. We translated those into wireframes to establish functionality, followed by the creation of design system components, and finished with high-fidelity mock ups. Between each of these phases we met with our client's legal team to confirm adherence to FDA rules and regulations.

Strategy

For ease of navigation and a customizable flow, each screen of the app would feature top-level takeaways with additional supporting data or graphics accessible via screen overlays. This would allow reps to keep their conversations high-level, while having the ability to dig deeper on each topic if the doctor they were speaking with wanted to.

Wireframes

I created wireframes for every screen within each section to illustrate our proposed structure and functionality to the client. We sent the wireframes through their legal review to ensure our proposed design was acceptable before moving forward into higher-fidelity layouts.

Functionality

Some of the functional requirements were dictated by our client to keep the app consistent with other tools their reps were familiar with, while others were developed by our team to streamline the app's interface.

  1. Forward and backward buttons allowing reps to jump between top-level content sections
  2. Button to access a menu of top-level content sections
  3. Tapping brand logo brings user home
  4. On-screen tabs to separate content
  5. Zoom button to open a screen overlay to dive deeper into data, graphs, or charts
  6. Button to open screen overlay for additional content
  7. Button to display visual map of all content areas, including overlays
  8. Button to access screen overlay that summarizes key takeaways

Necessary on-screen elements

There were a few elements each screen needed to have to pass our client's legal review. I decided to keep all of this contained at the bottom of each screen.

  1. Any applicable footnotes
  2. Important safety information related to that screen's particular content
  3. Ability to access the drug's prescribing information at all times
  4. Any applicable journal references accessible at all times

Click/tap behavior

Analyzing click/tap behavior indicated that users were unclear where to tap on the page. The CTAs on the page were rarely interacted with.

  • 53% of users clicked the brand logo, causing the page to reload
  • <1% clicked on the CTAs to expand the primary navigation which serves as the gateway into the cvaoma-specific pages of the website

Landscape analysis

We analyzed three multi-treatment cancer drug websites to better understand how products with similar requirements went about ensuring their users were able to access the content relevant to them.

We discovered three models:

1. Tile

Tiles look like buttons, have instructional copy, and are clearly clickable

2. Dropdown

Clear instructional copy prompts users to engage with dropdown menu

3. Multiple entry point

In-page CTA and global nav provide two places for users to select cancer type and proceed into interior pages

Proposed design solutions

Scroll, tap/click, and landscape analyses in hand, it was time to synthsize this research into some low-fidelity layouts to test on real users.

I created three options:

1. Tiles

  1. Prominent copy in the hero space explaining what the drug is used to treat
  2. cvaoma type selector located in primary navigation bar
  3. Instructional copy guiding the user how to engage with the site
  4. All treatments visible on the landing page, organized into cvaoma type

2. Dropdown

  1. Prominent copy in the hero space explaining what the drug is used to treat
  2. cvaoma type selector located in primary navigation bar
  3. Large CTA button guiding user to open the dropdown menu and select their cvaoma type
  4. Within the dropdown menu, all treatments are organized into cvaoma type

3. Balance of both

  1. Prominent copy in the hero space explaining what the drug is used to treat
  2. cvaoma type selector located in primary navigation bar
  3. Prominent CTA button providing clear instruction to users about how to engage with the site; clicking the button autoscrolls the user down to cvaoma-type selection CTA buttons.
  4. All treatments are organized into cvaoma type

Remote user testing

Our client had limited budget for user testing, so we decided to use UsabilityHub, which is an inexpensive, remote testing tool. We created a preference test to help validate which was the preferred design solution.

Testing population

  • 50 users from the general public (not actual cancer patients)
  • Individuals in the key patient age demographic (20-35 and 65+ years old)
  • Individuals who had low to intermediate technical proficiency since many of the drug's target demographic may be older and less familiar with navigating the web

Results

Of the three solutions proposed, a statistically significant percentage of users preferred solution 3: balance of both. This was our client's preferred design solution as well.

With the client's blessing we proceeded into full layout design, creating high fidelity desktop and mobile mock ups to be developed and pushed live.
