Our client, a cancer drug manufacturer, tasked the team with improving the usabilty and reducing the bouce rates of their landing page. Using a combination of qualitative and quantitative research I designed a new, better-performing version.

All content has been blinded to maintain client confidentiality.

Problem

Data pulled from Google Analytics and Hotjar showed that users were not moving beyond the homepage and were instead scrolling up and down the page before leaving the site all together (bouncing). The homepage also didn't feature any copy describing what the drug is used for. I focused on mobile first since that was where the majority of site visits were originating.

Solution

I relied on heatmap, click/tap tracking, and user testing data to inform my redesign of the landing page. The chosen design solution better communicates intended user behavior which ultimately reduces bounce and ensures users find the content they are looking for.

Research

I utilized Hotjar to track user click/tap and scroll behavior, explored the competitive landscape to evaluate how other multi-condition medications designed their websites to help users navigate, as well as conducted remote user testing with UsabilityHub.

Scroll behavior

Analyzing scroll behavior on Hotjar led me to believe that users were confused about how to interact with the page and resorted to scrolling to hunt for a solution:

  • 66% of users began scrolling within 6 seconds, assuring me visitors were indeed scrolling down the page
  • 74% of users scrolled past the average page fold, thereby seeing any content that was not immediately visible upon landing on the page
  • 34% of users would scroll to the bottom of the page and then scroll back to the top, suggesting two possibilities:
    • They could not find relevant content to engage with
    • They were unclear what to do, implying ineffective use of call to actions (CTAs) on the page
  • 97% of users would bounce without interacting with the site in any form

Click/tap behavior

Analyzing click/tap behavior indicated that users were unclear where to tap on the page. The CTAs on the page were rarely interacted with.

  • 53% of users clicked the brand logo, causing the page to reload
  • <1% clicked on the CTAs to expand the primary navigation which serves as the gateway into the lymphoma-specific pages of the website

Landscape analysis

I analyzed three multi-treatment cancer drug websites to better understand how products with similar requirements went about ensuring their users were able to access the content relevant to them.

I discovered three models:

1. Tile

Tiles look like buttons, have instructional copy, and are clearly clickable

2. Dropdown

Clear instructional copy prompts users to engage with dropdown menu

3. Multiple entry point

In-page CTA and global nav provide two places for users to select cancer type and proceed into interior pages

Proposed design solutions

Scroll, tap/click, and landscape analyses in hand, it was time to synthsize this research into some low-fidelity layouts to test on real users.

I created three options:

1. Tiles

  1. Prominent copy in the hero space explaining what the drug is used to treat
  2. Lymphoma type selector located in primary navigation bar
  3. Instructional copy guiding the user how to engage with the site
  4. All treatments visible on the landing page, organized into lymphoma type

2. Dropdown

  1. Prominent copy in the hero space explaining what the drug is used to treat
  2. Lymphoma type selector located in primary navigation bar
  3. Large CTA button guiding user to open the dropdown menu and select their lymphoma type
  4. Within the dropdown menu, all treatments are organized into lymphoma type

3. Balance of both

  1. Prominent copy in the hero space explaining what the drug is used to treat
  2. Lymphoma type selector located in primary navigation bar
  3. Prominent CTA button providing clear instruction to users about how to engage with the site; clicking the button autoscrolls the user down to lymphoma-type selection CTA buttons.
  4. All treatments are organized into lymphoma type

Remote user testing

Our client had limited budget for user testing, so I decided to use UsabilityHub, which is an inexpensive, remote testing tool. I created a preference test to help validate which was the preferred design solution.

Testing population

  • 50 users from the general public (not actual cancer patients)
  • Individuals in the key patient age demographic (20-35 and 65+ years old)
  • Individuals who had low to intermediate technical proficiency since many of the drug's target demographic may be older and less familiar with navigating the web

Results

Of the three solutions proposed, a statistically significant percentage of users preferred solution 3: balance of both. This was our client's preferred design solution as well.

With the client's blessing I proceeded into full layout design, creating high fidelity desktop and mobile mock ups to be developed and pushed live.

Final design

The final design contained slight variations from the proposed wireframes due to client input, namely the exclusion of the primary instructional CTA button which was traded out for instructional copy. The client also asked that I include a new patient video section to the homepage below the hero space.

Final design performance

The latest analytics show a marked improvement in site performance:

  • Bounce rate -40%
  • Average time spent on site +36%
  • Engagement with homepage CTA buttons +20%
