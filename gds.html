At Evoke Giant, an advertising agency in the biotechnology and pharmaceutical space, I designed a fictional website to serve as an example of applying web design best practices and adherence to a design system.

Problem

Evoke Giant was looking for guidance in best practices when designing biotech and pharma product websites to ensure industry standard design solutions were being utilized.

Solution

The website and accompanying design system contain some of the most common components and patterns used in biotech and pharma product websites. They were assembled using Brad Frost’s Atomic Design Methodology and were optimized for both desktop and mobile.

Components

I completed an analysis and compiled a list of design elements used in biotechnology and pharmaceutical websites both those done by Evoke Giant, as well as brands worked on by competitor agencies. I worked closely with our UX strategist and director of UX to focus the list of components needed.

Typography

I established a typographic heirarchy that could be used across any project. It provides an example of appropriate type sizing, agnostic of typeface, font weight, and case.

Color

I created a color palette that expands upon Evoke Giant’s corporate colors. It’s meant to serve as an example for color selection.

Icons

Icons can be incorporated throughout a design to provide a visual representation to users of what they are seeing and interacting with. This collection of icons serves as a small start to a full icon library.

Buttons

Buttons are one of the primary way users interact with a website. It's best for a button to contain clear copy and ideally be combined with an icon. Doing so helps the user understand what output to expect after pressing it.

Common design patterns

I came up with solutions for some of the most frequently encountered design problems in pharma and biotech websites, as well as how they translate to mobile. Often the transition to mobile is responsive, but occasionally it must be adaptive.

Tabs

Tabs can be used to display multiple blocks of data, be it charts or graphs. Using accordions on mobile is an appropriate adaptive solution.

References and switches

1. References as tooltips

Pharmaceutical companies rely on peer-reviewed research to justify the efficacy claims they make. Clickable tooltips are a great method for providing instant access to these references; they also translate responsively to mobile.

2. Toggle switches

Switches are useful for toggling between two sets of data, two charts, or really any two elements that belong together. They can be converted responsively or adaptively as accordions in mobile.

Carousels

Carousels are used to show a large collection of similar content, such as a library of videos. They translate responsively to mobile as long as the design makes clear through visual cues that the user can swipe to see more content.

Primary navigation menu

A large dropdown menu on desktop can be used to show a robust or complex collection of subpages. When translated to mobile, accordion functionality is a nice solution.

Next Steps

When building a design system at an agency, every new client project provides an opportunity to expand upon it as new projects require additional design solutions. These new components and patterns will be folded into additional pages of the fictional site to keep it, and the accompanying design system, current and continually evolving.
