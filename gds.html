At Evoke Giant, an advertising agency in the biotechnology and pharmaceutical spce, I helped create a design system that could be used as a jumping off point for digital projects. This system consists of some of the most common components used in healthcare and pharma websites.

Problem

Evoke Giant was looking for guidance in best practices when designing for websites to ensure industry standard design solutions were being utilized. It would allow their art teams to focus their energy on creative concepting and bringing their client’s brands to life without the need to worry about usability heuristics and web design standards.

Solution

I created what I’ve called the Giant Design System — or GDS. It’s a library of the most common design components used in healthcare and pharma websites assembled using Brad Frost’s Atomic Design Methodology. It’s designed in such a way that the elements can be mixed and matched to come up with new creative website layouts. Each component can be visually personalized to meet each client’s brand needs, while the underlying functionality stays the same.

Components

I completed an analysis and compiled a list of design elements used in biotechnology and pharmaceutical websites both those done by Evoke Giant, as well as brands worked on by competitor agencies. I worked closely with our UX strategist and director of UX to focus the list of components needed for the initial launch of GDS.

Typography

I established a typographic heirarchy for the GDS that could be used across any project. It provides an example of appropriate type sizing, agnostic of typeface, font weight, and case.

Color

I created a color palette to be used for the GDS that expands upon Evoke Giant’s corporate colors. It’s meant to serve as an example for color selection and will obviously be replaced by a client’s own brand colors.

Icons

Icons can be incorporated throughout a design to provide a visual representation to users of what they are seeing and interacting with. This collection of icons is just the beginning of a robust icon library.

Buttons

Buttons are one of the primary way users interact with your site. When combined with icons, they provide a clear visual representation of what the user can expect when pressing them.

Common design patterns

I created designs for some of the most frequently used design patterns, including how they translate to mobile. Often the transition to mobile is a responsive solution, but occasionally it must be adaptive.

Tabs and tables

1. Tabs and accordions

Tabs are a common solution for displaying multiple blocks of data, though often don't translate well to mobile. Using accordions on mobile are an appropriate adaptive solution.

2. Tables

Tables are necessary for displaying data on pharmaceutical websites. They also translate well to mobile with little issue.

References and switches

1. References as tooltips

Pharmaceutical companies rely on peer-reviewed research to justify the efficacy claims they make. Clickable tooltips are a great method for providing instant access to these references; they also translate responsively to mobile.

2. Toggle switches

Switches are useful for toggling between two sets of data, two charts, or really any two elements that belong together. They can be converted responsively or adaptively as accordions in mobile.

Carousels

1. Carousels

Carousels are used to show a large collection of similar content, such as a library of videos. They translate responsively to mobile as long as the design makes clear through the use of breadcrumbs and content that bleeds off the screen that there is more to be seen.

Menus

1. Menus

A large dropdown menu on desktop, sometimes called a mega menu, can be used to show a robust or complex collection of subpages. When translated to mobile, accordion functionality is a nice solution.

Next Steps

The GDS is a living, breathing system that will never truly be finished. It must grow and adapt as the needs of the business, and our clients, evolve. What is amazing about building a design system at an agency is that for every new client project, there's an opportunity to expand upon the system as we take into account the unique needs of each client.

Expanded Component and Icon Library

One area of the Giant Design System that will continually grow is our library of components and icons. There are near endless combinations of ways to create new design components and accompanying icons, and as the agency gets confronted with new design challenges from clients, our libraries will continue to expand.

Translation into Code

I have begun working with our development team to translate the design system's elements into coded components in our agency’s React codebase. Having the functionality and layout of each component coded out ahead of time will save the dev team massive amounts of time when building new projects since they simply need to adjust the styling of each component as opposed to building it from scratch.

Incorporate Usage Rules

Creating the usage documentation for the design system is needed next. This will ensure that anyone looking to borrow elements from the system incorporates them correctly into their designs.
